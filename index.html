Superhero Long War

Roguelike Deckbuilder Design Specification

XCOM meets Slay the Spire in a world under siege

Overview

Concept: A roguelike deckbuilder combining XCOM-style strategic layer with Slay the Spire combat. Players manage a superhero organization defending the remnants of America against three hostile factions.

Core Parameters

Parameter Value
Target Campaign Length ~10 hours
Heroes per Combat 3
Cards per Hero 5-10
Combined Deck Size 15-30 cards
Max Roster Size 10-12 heroes
Campaign Acts 3

Core Loop

Strategic Map Management ↓ Mission Selection ↓ Deckbuilder Combat ↓ Rewards / Recovery ↓ Repeat

Setting

The player runs a Justice League-style superhero organization. The Midwestern United States represents the last bastion of humanity, besieged on all sides by three hostile factions that emerged from "the Veil"—a tear in reality through which nightmarish threats pour.

There is no winning this war permanently. The best the player can hope for is to prevent each faction's apocalyptic plans as they arise, buying humanity another day to survive.

Enemy Factions

All three factions originate from the Veil and remain active throughout the campaign. Each has distinct aesthetics, mechanics, and territory.

Cult of the Veil

Territory: Southern States

Theme: Lovecraftian horrors, reality-warping entities, fanatical cultists

Combat Style: Psychic attacks, debuffs, sanity erosion, summoning rituals

Signature Mechanics:

  • Apply stacking mental debuffs that trigger at thresholds
  • Summon increasingly powerful entities over time
  • Cards that punish large hands or specific card types

The Undead Horde

Territory: Eastern States

Theme: Necromancers, revenants, death knights, skeletal legions

Combat Style: Attrition warfare, life drain, resurrection, inevitability

Signature Mechanics:

  • Enemies resurrect unless killed with specific damage types
  • Life drain heals the enemy while damaging heroes
  • Death triggers that strengthen remaining enemies

The Swarm

Territory: Western States

Theme: Alien bugs (Zerg-style), hive minds, overwhelming numbers

Combat Style: Poison, swarm mechanics, rapid spawning, sacrifice tactics

Signature Mechanics:

  • Rapidly spawn weak enemies that buff each other
  • Stacking poison that grows each turn
  • Sacrifice smaller units to empower larger ones

Campaign Structure

The campaign follows a three-act structure similar to Slay the Spire, but with rotating faction focus:

Act 1

One faction pursues an apocalyptic plan. The player must stop it via story missions.

Act 2

A different faction's apocalyptic plan becomes the primary threat.

Act 3

The third faction's plan threatens to end everything.

Important: All factions remain active throughout the entire campaign. The act structure only determines which faction's story missions are currently relevant. Defense missions from any faction can occur at any time.

Campaign End: After completing Act 3, the campaign ends. There is no "happily ever after"—just "the immediate crisis is over, the war continues." The ending reflects the reality that this is a holding action, not a victory.

Strategic Resources

Money

Universal spending currency for the strategic layer.

  • Generated by: Controlled territories, mission rewards
  • Spent at: The Bazaar (recruitment, upgrades, training)

Time

Implicit resource that creates strategic tension.

  • Heroes are unavailable during travel, training, and recovery
  • Missions have expiration timers
  • Story missions must be completed before doom counter fills

Personnel

Your hero roster—the most valuable resource.

  • Limited by barracks capacity
  • Permanently lost on death
  • Each hero represents significant investment

Territories

Your holdings on the strategic map.

  • Generate money and may have special bonuses
  • Define your defensive perimeter
  • Can be lost to enemy attacks

Strategic Map

Territory Overview

Player-Controlled 4-7 at any time

Player holdings are clustered together. The borders with enemy territory form "front lines" where defense missions spawn.

Landmark Territories

Special territories that provide unique benefits and are high-priority targets for enemies:

Landmark Benefit

The Sanctuary Faster stress recovery for heroes
The Listening Post Advance warning of incoming attacks

Faction Heat System

Each faction has a heat meter representing their aggression toward the player organization.

Heat Mechanics

  • Increased by: Actions against that faction (story missions, killing their units)
  • Decreased by: Successful defense against that faction's attacks
  • Effect: Higher heat = more frequent attacks from that faction

Design Purpose

The heat system prevents "ignore two factions, crush one" strategies. Players must manage attention across all three threats, creating a juggling dynamic where focusing too heavily on one faction causes the others to become more dangerous.

Mission Types

Defense Missions

  • Spawn at front lines when a faction attacks
  • Single combat encounter
  • Failure: Lose the territory

Story Missions

  • 2-3 combat encounters in sequence
  • Faction-specific narrative content
  • Advance the current act's storyline
  • Failure: Advances doom counter; can retry until mission expires
  • Expiration: Game over

Capture Missions

  • Opportunistic expansion missions
  • Spawn occasionally on the map edge
  • Taking new territory extends your defensive perimeter
  • Risk/reward: More territory = more resources but harder to defend

Doom Counter

Each act has a doom counter tied to the featured faction's apocalyptic plan.

  • Story mission failures advance the doom counter
  • If the counter fills completely, game over
  • Functions as a "retry budget" for story missions
  • Creates tension: How many times can you afford to fail?

Bazaar System

The Bazaar refreshes monthly with randomized purchasable options:

  • New Heroes: Arrive with some cards pre-loaded
  • Techniques/Strategies: Organizational upgrades
  • Training Opportunities: Ways to upgrade existing heroes

Design Note: The randomized selection creates "buy now or wait" decisions. This is not a tech tree—it's a roulette. Sometimes the perfect recruit appears when you're broke. Sometimes you have money but nothing good is available.

Training

  • Heroes can train to upgrade cards in their deck
  • Training costs money and makes the hero unavailable
  • Upgrades are randomized "proposals"—player picks from options
  • Core Tradeoff: Train your best heroes (lose access to them) or train B-listers?

Hero System

Heroes are the core asset of your organization. Each hero is a significant investment that can be permanently lost.

Hero Components

  • Class: Determines card pool and combat role
  • Personal Deck: 5-10 cards unique to that hero
  • Stress Level: Accumulates with deployment
  • Trauma Cards: Permanent penalties from breakdowns
  • Resistances: Faction-specific tolerances

Hero Decks

Each hero maintains an individual deck:

  • Starting Size: 5 cards (4 basics + 1 random common)
  • Maximum Size: ~10 cards
  • Growth: Card rewards after missions (not individual combats)
  • Two heroes of the same class play similarly but not identically

Hero Classes

Classes are based on Worm power classifications. Each class has a distinct card pool, primary/secondary resource scaling, and can generate any of the five combat resources.

Brute

Tank and damage dealer. High health pool with cards that absorb hits and punish enemies for attacking.

Role Tank / Damage
Primary Scaling Blood
Secondary Scaling Iron

Playstyle: Absorb damage, build up resources through punishment, unleash devastating counterattacks.

Blaster

Ranged damage dealer. Straightforward offensive powerhouse with energy projection.

Role Damage Dealer
Primary Scaling Ashes
Secondary Scaling Blood

Playstyle: Direct damage, area attacks, building toward massive finisher moves.

Tinker

Buildup class with gadgets and persistent effects. Slow start but extremely powerful late in combat.

Role Buildup / Control
Primary Scaling Pages
Secondary Scaling Glint

Playstyle: Deploy gadgets and constructs early, gain compounding advantages, dominate long fights.

Thinker

Card manipulation and support. Weak solo but massive force multiplier for the team.

Role Support / Control
Primary Scaling Glint
Secondary Scaling Pages

Playstyle: Draw cards, scry, manipulate the deck, set up perfect turns for allies.

Mover

Positioning and evasion specialist. Avoids attacks and redirects enemy attention.

Role Evasion / Utility
Primary Scaling Glint
Secondary Scaling Iron

Playstyle: Dodge attacks entirely, reposition threats, protect vulnerable allies through misdirection.

Stranger

Stealth and misdirection master. Reduces threat profile and strikes from ambush.

Role Stealth / Burst
Primary Scaling Ashes
Secondary Scaling Glint

Playstyle: Stay hidden, build up ambush bonuses, strike for massive damage when the moment is right.

Roster Management

Parameter Value
Barracks Cap 10-12 heroes maximum
Starting Roster TBD (fixed, randomized, or draft)
Starting Deck per Hero 4 basics + 1 random common

Recruitment

New heroes are recruited via the Bazaar. They arrive with cards pre-loaded (4 basics + random common cards based on their class).

Permadeath

Heroes can die in combat. Death is permanent—there is no resurrection, no second chances. The investment in that hero's deck, training, and experience is lost.

Dismissal

Players may need to dismiss heroes to make room for new recruits. This creates difficult decisions about roster composition.

Stress & Trauma System

Stress

  • Accumulates per deployment and based on combat events
  • Prevents overusing your "A-team"
  • High stress increases risk of breakdown

Breakdown

If a hero is pushed too far, they suffer a breakdown and gain a permanent Trauma card added to their deck.

Trauma Cards

  • Function like curse cards—negative effects when drawn
  • Varied effects (specific effects TBD)
  • Cannot be removed—accumulated trauma is permanent
  • Eventually makes heroes less viable over time

Design Purpose

Creates character arcs: Veterans are powerful but carry scars. A hero who has survived dozens of missions will have a larger, more powerful deck but also accumulated trauma that weighs them down. Eventually, even the greatest heroes become liabilities.

Faction Resistances

Heroes develop resistances through exposure to specific factions:

  • A veteran cult-fighter develops mental fortitude against psychic attacks
  • A bug specialist builds toxin tolerance against Swarm poison
  • An undead hunter gains death's defiance against life drain

Design Purpose: Creates "who to send" decisions beyond raw power. Your strongest hero might not be the best choice for a Cult mission if they have no mental resistance. Specialization matters.

Combat System

Slay the Spire-style combat with merged hero decks.

Core Structure

  1. Take 3 heroes into combat
  2. Mash all three decks together into one combined draw pile
  3. Draw a hand, spend energy, play cards
  4. Enemies telegraph their intents
  5. End turn—enemies act
  6. Repeat until victory or defeat

Key Distinction: This is a shared turn, not per-hero turns. All heroes act simultaneously, and any card in hand can be played regardless of which hero it belongs to.

Deck Composition

  • Combined Size: 15-30 cards (3 heroes × 5-10 cards each)
  • Small enough for consistency
  • Large enough for variety and strategic deck-building

Combat Resources

Five abstract resources that persist across turns (similar to Strength in StS, but more central to gameplay):

Blood

Vitality, sacrifice, cost. Associated with life force and paying prices for power.

Glint

Perception, opportunity, insight. Associated with awareness and seizing moments.

Ashes

Destruction, entropy, aftermath. Associated with damage dealt and things destroyed.

Pages

Knowledge, preparation, plans. Associated with information and setup.

Iron

Will, endurance, resolve. Associated with defense and persistence.

Resource System Design

Key Principle: Generation is Distributed, Scaling is Specialized

Generation: Any class can generate any resource. Generator cards exist across all classes, often as card upgrades.

Scaling: Class-specific. Each class scales 1-2 resources. Cards read "Deal 5 + Blood damage" or "Gain 4 + Iron block."

This creates team composition decisions: Match generators to scalers. A Brute who scales Blood wants teammates who can generate Blood.

Baseline Resource Sinks

Each resource has a universal ability available to any team (costs 1 energy + 3 resource):

Resource Baseline Ability
Blood Deal 8 damage to any enemy
Glint Draw 2 cards
Ashes Exhaust a card from hand
Pages [TBD]
Iron Gain 8 block

Card Design Principles

No Dead Cards

Every card does something useful independent of resources. Resources provide bonuses, not gates.

Example Card Patterns

  • "Deal 6 damage. If you have 3+ Blood, deal 10 instead."
  • "Gain 5 block. Spend up to 3 Iron: gain 2 block per Iron spent."
  • "Draw 2 cards. +1 card per Glint."

No Orphaned Resources

The baseline sink abilities ensure every resource has value, even if no hero on the team specializes in it.

Card Upgrades

Monster Train style: Cards can have upgrades "baked in" when acquired. The player doesn't choose upgrades—the card comes with them pre-applied.

Example Upgrade Types

  • "This card generates 2 Blood."
  • "This card generates 3 Glint then consumes."
  • "This card costs 1 less energy."
  • "This card has +3 base damage."

Thinning Policy

Limited or no deck thinning. At 5-10 card deck sizes per hero, thinning would be too powerful.

Options under consideration:

  • No thinning (decks only grow)
  • Very limited thinning (rare/expensive)
  • Transformation only (upgrade basics, can't delete)

Hero Death in Combat

When a hero falls mid-combat:

  • Their cards stop appearing in future draws
  • Cards already in hand or discard remain (become dead draws)
  • Combat continues with remaining heroes

Timing Matters: Losing someone early in combat is devastating—their entire deck becomes dead draws. Losing someone late may barely matter if most of their cards have already been played.

Retreat & Pyrrhic Victories

Retreat Mechanics

  • Costs 3 energy to initiate
  • Enemies get their telegraphed attack (one final hit)
  • Surviving team members take stress
  • Available during story missions—can retreat from one combat and attempt the next, or abort entirely

Pyrrhic Victories

The combat system is designed for "rubber-banding"—partial success states:

  • Losing a hero doesn't automatically mean losing the combat
  • Surviving with losses is a valid (and sometimes optimal) outcome
  • Creates dramatic "we won, but at what cost" moments

Design Goal: Combat should feel tense throughout, with meaningful decisions about when to play it safe vs. push for advantage. Losing should hurt but not immediately end the run.

Card Rewards

  • Granted per mission, not per individual combat
  • After mission completion, each participating hero gets a choice
  • Choice format: Pick from 3 randomized bundles
  • Bundle contents: Card(s) + possibly passive buffs
  • Available cards based on hero's class

Design Pillars

1. Meaningful Loss

Death, trauma, and territory loss matter but don't end runs immediately. With ~10 hour campaigns, losses sting without being hours of wasted investment. The war continues even when heroes fall.

2. Team Composition Matters

Resource generation/scaling, faction resistances, and stress management make "who to send" a real decision. The right team for a mission isn't always your strongest heroes.

3. Strategic Triage

You can't be everywhere. Heat management, doom counters, and limited personnel force hard choices. Sometimes you have to let a territory fall to save something more important.

4. No Dead Draws

Every card does something. Resources enhance, not gate. You should never draw a card and feel it's completely useless in the current situation.

5. Emergent Narrative

Veteran heroes accumulate trauma. Territories fall and are retaken. The campaign tells a story through systems, not just scripted events. Your war will be different from someone else's war.

UI/UX Notes

Resource Display

  • Color-coded resource icons
  • Cards show scaling in resource colors: "Deal 5 + [3]" where red = Blood, 3 = current value
  • Generation shown with +, consumption with -, scaling with =
  • Deck viewer shows resource profile at a glance

Combat

  • Enemy intents visible (Slay the Spire style)
  • Combined deck is visible and countable
  • Resource pools clearly displayed
  • Baseline resource abilities accessible as buttons

Strategic Map

  • Territory control visible at a glance
  • Faction heat meters displayed prominently
  • Active missions clearly marked with timers
  • Hero availability status immediately apparent
